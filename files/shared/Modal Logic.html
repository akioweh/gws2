Modal Logic

Read the intro of First-order Logic for a more abstract view of how Formal Systems and Logics are defined and characterized.

Modal Logic is a class of Formal (Logic) Systems.

In general, a Modal Language is the language of Propositional Logic extended with new unary connectives – modalities. They express the notion of “possibility”.

Then, the Rules of Inference of a modal language may vary across different compositions of axioms, or conditions on frames (name for modal-structures).

Here we discuss “Standard”/“Normal” Regular Modal Logic, which has two modalities: ◊\lozenge◊, POSSIBLY; and □\square□, NECESSARILY.

As a Logic, Modal Logic is considered non-classical, whereas Propositional and First-order Logics are classical.

Semantics

Specifically, we’re discussing Kripke semantics (aka. relational semantics or frame semnatics).

Modal Structures

An L\mathcal{L}L-structure, aka. Modal / Kripke Frame, is a pair M=⟨W,R⟩M = \braket{W, R}M=⟨W,R⟩:

  • WWW – the Universe
    a (possibly empty) set of worlds
  • RRR – Accessibility Relation
    a binary relation on WWW
    for w1,w2∈Ww_1, w_2 \in Ww1​,w2​∈W,  (w1,w2)∈R \ (w_1, w_2) \in R \space (w1​,w2​)∈R  iff  w2\ w_2 w2​ is accessible from w1w_1w1​ 1

In Graph Theory, MMM is a directed graph with vertices WWW and edges RRR.
Intuitively, it is a map of worlds and how they’re interconnected.

Formulas are interpreted with respect to (“in”) a given world. A world represents a state of truth – i.e., the trueness of a proposition is dependent on the world it’s in.
The “neighbors” of a world (as given by RRR) are the possibilities as seen from said world. This is how “necessity” and “possibility” are formally treated.

Valuations

As an L\mathcal{L}L-structure can have multiple worlds, a valuation does not directly assign propositions truth values – rather, specifies which propositions are true in which worlds.

A valuation in an L\mathcal{L}L-structure ⟨WM,RM⟩\braket{W^M, R^M}⟨WM,RM⟩ is a mapping
ρ:WM×P↦{⊤,⊥} \rho : W^M \times \mathcal{P} \mapsto \{ \top, \bot \} ρ:WM×P↦{⊤,⊥}

where ρ(w,P)=⊤ \rho(w, P) = \top \spaceρ(w,P)=⊤  iff  P\ P P is true in world www.

Some instead have ρ:P↦WM\rho : \mathcal{P} \mapsto W^Mρ:P↦WM – i.e., ρ(P)\rho(P)ρ(P) is the set of worlds where PPP is true.

Combined, we get an interpretation / relational model M=⟨W,R,ρ⟩\mathfrak{M} = \braket{W, R, \rho}M=⟨W,R,ρ⟩.

Then, the evaluation of a formula at a world www in a model M\mathfrak{M}M is defined inductively.
For P∈PP \in \mathcal{P}P∈P; φ,ψ∈L\varphi, \psi \in \mathcal{L}φ,ψ∈L; and binary connective op\text{op}op,
M,w⊨P  ⟺  ρ(w,P)M,w⊨¬φ  ⟺  w⊭φM,w⊨(φ  op  ψ)  ⟺  (w⊨φ)  op  (w⊨ψ)M,w⊨□φ  ⟺  ∀u∈WM:wRMu→u⊨φM,w⊨◊φ  ⟺  ∃u∈WM:wRMu∧u⊨φ \begin{align*} \mathfrak{M}, w \models P &\iff \rho(w, P) \\ \mathfrak{M}, w \models \lnot \varphi &\iff w \not \models \varphi \\ \mathfrak{M}, w \models (\varphi \;\text{op}\; \psi) &\iff (w \models \varphi) \;\text{op}\; (w \models \psi) \\ \mathfrak{M}, w \models \square \varphi &\iff \forall u \in W^\mathfrak{M} : w R^\mathfrak{M} u \rightarrow u \models \varphi \\ \mathfrak{M}, w \models \lozenge \varphi &\iff \exists u \in W^\mathfrak{M} : w R^\mathfrak{M} u \land u \models \varphi \\ \end{align*} M,w⊨PM,w⊨¬φM,w⊨(φopψ)M,w⊨□φM,w⊨◊φ​⟺ρ(w,P)⟺w⊨φ⟺(w⊨φ)op(w⊨ψ)⟺∀u∈WM:wRMu→u⊨φ⟺∃u∈WM:wRMu∧u⊨φ​

Alternative notation for M,w⊨φ\mathfrak{M}, w \models \varphiM,w⊨φ (specifying both a structure and a valuation) includes M,w⊨ρφM, w \models_\rho \varphiM,w⊨ρ​φ, which is more explicit about the valuation.

The intuitive implications are clear:

  • a statement is necessary (from world www) if it holds in every world accessible from www;
  • a statement is possible (from world www) if it holds in at least one world accessible from www.

Satisfaction Relation (Validity & Satisfiability)

A definition of ⊨\models⊨ as used above. It is called a satisfaction relation as it denotes the satisfaction (trueful-evaluation) of a formula under an interpretation.

Some authors use ⊩\Vdash⊩, defined within M\mathfrak{M}M as an equivalent replacement for ρ\rhoρ, instead of the meta-language symbol ⊨\models⊨.2
We use subscript notation, ⊨ρ\models_\rho⊨ρ​, to achieve the same effect.

For valuation ρ\rhoρ for frame M=⟨W,R⟩M = \braket{W, R}M=⟨W,R⟩ and w∈Ww \in Ww∈W,

“φ\varphiφ is true (under ρ\rhoρ) at www (in MMM)”
  ⟺  M,w⊨ρφ\iff M, w \models_\rho \varphi⟺M,w⊨ρ​φ

“φ\varphiφ is valid in MMM”
  ⟺  M⊨φ\iff M \models \varphi⟺M⊨φ

“φ\varphiφ is valid”
  ⟺  ⊨φ\iff \models \varphi⟺⊨φ
∀M:M⊨φ\forall M : M \models \varphi∀M:M⊨φ

“φ\varphiφ is satisfiable in MMM”
  ⟺  ∃ρ ∃w:M,w⊨ρφ\iff \exists \rho\, \exists w : M, w \models_\rho \varphi⟺∃ρ∃w:M,w⊨ρ​φ

I.e., “φ\varphiφ is satisfiable in some frame”.

“φ\varphiφ is valid”   ⟺  \iff⟺ “¬φ\lnot \varphi¬φ is unsatisfiable”;

Validity   ⟹  \implies⟹ Validity in MMM   ⟹  \implies⟹ Satisfiability in MMM   ⟹  \implies⟹ Satisfiability.

Rules of Inference

One unique way axioms can be defined for modalities is via properties on RRR.
Observe from above how the modal connectives are directly related to the accessibility relation.
The rules imposed on RRR are called frame conditions.

Base Axioms

These hold true for all frames (in normal modal logics).

N – Necessitation
(⊨φ)  ⟹  (⊨□φ) (\models \varphi) \implies (\models \square \varphi) (⊨φ)⟹(⊨□φ)

I.e., if φ\varphiφ is a tautology, then so is □φ\square \varphi□φ.

K – Distribution
□(φ→ψ)  ⟹  (□φ→□ψ) \square (\varphi \rightarrow \psi) \implies (\square \varphi \rightarrow \square \psi) □(φ→ψ)⟹(□φ→□ψ)

Or, equivalently, □[(φ→ψ)∧φ]  ⟹  □ψ\square \big[(\varphi \rightarrow \psi) \land \varphi \big] \implies \square \psi□[(φ→ψ)∧φ]⟹□ψ, establishing Modus Ponens in modal logic.

Frame Conditions

Common frame conditions (their code names) and equivalent axioms:

  • T: Reflexive – ∀w:wRw\forall w : w R w∀w:wRw
    φ→◊φ \quad \varphi \rightarrow \lozenge \varphi \spaceφ→◊φ  (and so □φ→φ\square \varphi \rightarrow \varphi□φ→φ)
  • B: Symmetric – ∀w,u:wRu→uRw\forall w, u : w R u \rightarrow u R w∀w,u:wRu→uRw
    φ→□◊φ \quad \varphi \rightarrow \square\lozenge \varphi \spaceφ→□◊φ  (and so ◊□φ→φ\lozenge\square \varphi \rightarrow \varphi◊□φ→φ)
  • 4: Transitive – ∀w,u,q:wRu∧uRq→wRq\forall w, u, q : w R u \land u R q \rightarrow w R q∀w,u,q:wRu∧uRq→wRq
    □φ→□□φ\quad \square \varphi \rightarrow \square \square \varphi□φ→□□φ
  • D: Serial – ∀w ∃u:wRu\forall w\, \exists u : w R u∀w∃u:wRu
    □φ→◊φ\quad \square \varphi \rightarrow \lozenge \varphi□φ→◊φ
  • 5: Euclidean – ∀w,u,q:wRu∧wRq→uRq\forall w, u, q : w R u \land w R q \rightarrow u R q∀w,u,q:wRu∧wRq→uRq
    ◊φ→□◊φ\quad \lozenge \varphi \rightarrow \square\lozenge \varphi◊φ→□◊φ

(where the domain for the quantifiers is, of course, WWW.)
