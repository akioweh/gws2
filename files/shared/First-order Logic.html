First-order Logic

Recall the notes on Formal Language & Grammar; here we discuss a specialization of formal language that is capable of expressing first-order logic, a First-order Language.
From this, we also discuss interpretation in first-order languages and inference rules; together, one obtains a Formal System (of Logic).

Alphabet

Recall what an alphabet, Σ\SigmaΣ, is in a Grammar.

For a first-order language, we further subdivide Σ\SigmaΣ into ⟨λ,σ⟩\braket{\lambda, \sigma}⟨λ,σ⟩:

  • λ\lambdaλ – Logical Symbols
  • σ\sigmaσ – Nonlogical Symbols (aka. signature)

This division facilitates the separation of semantics from syntax (formulation rules).

Logical Symbols

Have constant, assumed, meaning (across all interpretations).
Part of Syntax

The exact contents of λ\lambdaλ is up to the author, although conventionally it contains:

  • Quantifiers
    • ∀\forall∀ – universal quantification
    • ∃\exists∃ – existential quantification
  • Logical Connectives (relations / operators)
    • ¬\lnot¬ – negation
    • ∧\land∧ – conjunction
    • ∨\lor∨ – disjunction
    • →\rightarrow→ – implication
    • ↔\leftrightarrow↔ – biimplication
  • Punctuation
    • (, ) – parenthesis, for precedence grouping
    • ., ,, etc. – other delimiting/grouping features
  • Constants (syntactically, also variables)
    • ⊤\top⊤ – trueness
    • ⊥\bot⊥ – falseness
  • Variables (stand-in symbols for statements)
    • an infinite set, V\mathcal{V}V, e.g., {a,b,c,d,… }\{ a, b, c, d, \dots \}{a,b,c,d,…}

An equality symbol, e.g., ===, is sometimes also included, or otherwise defined to be a nonlogical predicate.

Note that, especially the logical connectives and true and false constants, alternative symbols are often used to denote the same meaning. E.g., +++ for ∨\lor∨, ⋅\cdot⋅ for ∧\land∧, ∼\sim∼ for ¬\lnot¬, TTT for ⊤\top⊤, FFF for ⊥\bot⊥ .

Nonlogical Symbols

Semantics are assigned by interpretations
Part of Semantics

The author of the language defines the contents of σ=⟨F,P⟩\sigma = \braket{\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{P}}σ=⟨F,P⟩1:

  • F\mathcal{F}F – functions
  • P\mathcal{P}P – predicates (or RRR – relations)

“Constants”, should they be distinguished, are nullary functions. Similarly,
Propositions (from propositional logic) are nullary predicates.

RRR is isomorphic to P\mathcal{P}P; there is a bijection between the two forms.2

Another note, on Predicates vs Functions:
Behaviorally, predicates are just functions whose codomain is precisely {⊤,⊥}\{ \top, \bot \}{⊤,⊥}. (The inverse can also be achieved.3)
So there is no semantic difference.
In FOL, however, predicates and functions face different syntactic rules, and that’s why we segregate them here.

Note that σ\sigmaσ contains only “symbols” in the sense of representations of objects with unspecified meaning/behavior.
This is unlike λ\lambdaλ, whose contents do have their meaning/behavior specified.

To relate back to Formal Grammar, a first-order language L\mathcal{L}L, with the above contents of Σ\SigmaΣ, has L⊆Σ∗\mathcal{L} \subseteq \Sigma^*L⊆Σ∗

As the signature σ\sigmaσ is the only “variable” part of a first-order language, some consider it equivalent / definitive of a language.

Syntax (Language Formulation Rules)

Here we restate the grammar of first-order languages.

Everything discussed so far is only to establish the syntax of first-order languages.
Syntax allows the construction and manipulation of formulas without concern for what any of the nonlogical symbols represent.

Note that since λ\lambdaλ is not technically fixed, the following is not definitive. It is, however, what almost always is.

Semi-formal

Terms t∈Tt \in \mathcal{T}t∈T are (inductively defined as) any

  • v∈Vv \in \mathcal{V}v∈V
  • f(t1,…,tn)f(t_1, \dots,t_n)f(t1​,…,tn​) for f∈F, {t1,…,tn}⊆Tf \in \mathcal{F}, \ \{ t_1, \dots, t_n \} \subseteq \mathcal{T}f∈F, {t1​,…,tn​}⊆T

Atomic Formulas / atomic statements are any

  • P(t1,…,tn)P(t_1, \dots,t_n)P(t1​,…,tn​) for P∈P, {t1,…,tn}⊆TP \in \mathcal{P}, \ \{ t_1, \dots,t_n \} \subseteq \mathcal{T}P∈P, {t1​,…,tn​}⊆T
  • t1=t2t_1 = t_2t1​=t2​ for t1,t2∈Tt_1, t_2 \in \mathcal{T}t1​,t2​∈T4

(sentential) Formulas / statements are any

  • Atomic Formula
  • (for Formulas AAA, BBB and Logical Connective op\text{op}op)
    • A op B A \text{ op } B \spaceA op B  if op is binary
    • opA \text{op} A \spaceopA  if op is unary
  • (for Formula AAA and Quantifier Q\mathcal{Q}Q where xxx is unbound in AAA)
    • Qx A\mathcal{Q} x\, AQxA

Relating back to Formal Language, for any sentential formula AAA, A∈LA \in \mathcal{L}A∈L.

If we consider the “datatypes”, from computer science, of the above,
Terms are of Any type;
(Atomic) formulas are of boolean.

Every (sub-)formula is implicitly parenthesized.
For the final bullet point above, a ... or ::: may be added as Qx. A\mathcal{Q} x\mathbf{.}\, AQx.A or Qx: A\mathcal{Q} x\mathbf{:}\, AQx:A to more explicitly delimit the quantification clause from the sub-formula.

In actual writing, to reduce parenthesis, there is a conventional precedence for the logical connectives such that parenthesis may be omitted as long as unique readability is maintained. In descending precedence:

  1. ¬\lnot¬
  2. ∧\land∧ and ∨\lor∨
  3. ∀\forall∀ and ∃\exists∃
  4. →\rightarrow→ and ↔\leftrightarrow↔

Variable Binding

A variable is bound if it is quantified, and free otherwise.
Within a larger formula, a variable is unbound if it is free somewhere, and bound if bound everywhere.

A variable is quantified when it is used with a quantifier, e.g., C=(∀xA)∨BC = (\forall x A) \lor BC=(∀xA)∨B binds xxx in the scope of AAA, but is free in BBB, and is unbound in CCC.

Basically, bound variables are not “inputs” in the sense that they should have a value assigned to them (like propositions) to interpret the formula. They are implicit and cannot be assigned to.

Imagine quantifiers as a for-each loop, e.g., for (item : list){...}. Then, item is bound . (And as such you wouldn’t redefine it, since the loop “manages” it “automatically”.)

A formula with no (free) variables is a sentence.

Semantics (Interpretation Rules)

Specifically, we are discussing Tarskian semantics.

A language does not encode any semantics within itself.
Therefore, to perform any semantic treatment of a formula, we must interpret it.

An interpretation assigns a denotation to each nonlogical symbol.

There are two “parts” to an interpretation: a model and a valuation.

A model assigns values (behavior) to symbols from σ\sigmaσ.
A valuation exists under a model and assigns values to unbound variables from V\mathcal{V}V.

Note that although there are two “steps”, both are often called “interpretation” individually anyway (especially models).

Models

We interpret a first-order language in a first-order structure M=⟨D,I⟩M = \braket{\mathbb{D}, I}M=⟨D,I⟩5:

  • D\mathbb{D}D – Domain of Discourse / Universal Set
    • non-empty set
    • is the range of the quantifiers
    • I.e., are the “objects” the formula will “work with”
  • III – Interpretation Function
    • I:σ↦FM∪PMI: \sigma \mapsto \mathcal{F}^M \cup \mathcal{P}^MI:σ↦FM∪PM
    • I.e., maps symbols to their actual denoted objects
      • FM\mathcal{F}^MFM – nnn-ary functions of Dn↦D\mathbb{D}^n \mapsto \mathbb{D}Dn↦D
      • PM\mathcal{P}^MPM – nnn-ary predicates of Dn↦{⊤,⊥}\mathbb{D}^n \mapsto \{ \top, \bot \}Dn↦{⊤,⊥}

Note that assignability of a structure to a formula denotes whether the structure’s interpretation function covers all symbols in the formula.

Valuations

Valuation is the common technical term for “interpretation” (of a formula, in a model).
It more or less means to give meaning to variables (symbols in V\mathcal{V}V) until a formula in a structure can be transformed to one of {⊤,⊥}\{ \top, \bot \}{⊤,⊥}

Let ρ\rhoρ represent a valuation for a formula AAA in structure MMM. Then, there are several similar ways to define ρ\rhoρ.

  1. ρ\rhoρ is a “variable assignment” that associates an element of D\mathbb{D}D with each free variable in AAA. AAA is now a sentence and can now be be evaluated in MMM.

  2. ρ\rhoρ is a function that maps variables to values;
    ρ:V↦D \rho: \mathcal{V} \mapsto \mathbb{D} \spaceρ:V↦D 
    So by applying ρ\rhoρ to every free variable in AAA, we obtain a sentence.

  3. ρ\rhoρ is a “do-it-all” function that does both variable assignment and logical evaluation;
    ρ:L↦{⊤,⊥}\rho: \mathcal{L} \mapsto \{ \top, \bot \}ρ:L↦{⊤,⊥}
    such that ρ(A)\rho(A)ρ(A) directly gives us the result.

In the 3rd case, we may start from the definition of the 2nd case, then extend it to all terms, like:
ρ(f(t1,…,tn))=deffM(ρ(t1),…,ρ(tn))\rho(f(t_1, \dots, t_n)) =_{\text{def}} f^M(\rho(t_1), \dots, \rho(t_n))ρ(f(t1​,…,tn​))=def​fM(ρ(t1​),…,ρ(tn​))
where tnt_ntn​ are terms, and FM(f)=fM\mathcal{F}^M(f) = f^MFM(f)=fM.
Once we have terms, we “do the same things for atomic formulas and formulas and suddenly ρ\rhoρ is the do-it-all function”.

So, a formula can only be evaluated under a specified valuation in a specified structure (unless it is a tautology or contradiction).

Assignability here is similar to the assignability of a structure, though a valuation has to be compatible with the structure it is within, too.

Validity & Satisfiability

Satisfiable = could evaluate true
Valid = cannot evaluate false

Rules of Inference

Rules of Inferences are syntactical transformations, and as such operate only on symbols in λ\lambdaλ.

Axioms

Remember that in first-order logic, all variables are object variables serving as arguments of functions and predicates.
In second-order logic, variables may denote predicates (and as such predicates may be quantified).
We may write axioms for first-order logic using second-order logic (where the axioms are more precisely called axiom schemata).

The axiom schemata for first-order logic is those of propositional logic, plus the following two:
∀x A  ⟹  A[t/x]A[t/x]  ⟹  ∃x A \forall x \, A \implies A[t/x] \\[1em] A[t/x] \implies \exists x \, A ∀xA⟹A[t/x]A[t/x]⟹∃xA

where AAA is a Formula in which x∈Vx \in \mathcal{V}x∈V (exists and) is free, and t∈Tt \in \mathcal{T}t∈T. See below for the square bracket syntax.

I.e.,
If A is true for everything assigned to x, then we may assign anything to x and A will still be true.
If A is ever true, then there must exist something that when assigned to x makes A true

Substitution

A[t/x]A[t/x]A[t/x] means formula AAA with all (unbound) instances of variable xxx replaced by term ttt. I.e., a refactoring.

ttt should not contain variable symbols already in AAA as that might cause variable shadowing and corrupt the formula.

Equivalences

A≡B  ⟺  ∀M, ∀ρ, (M⊨ρA)⇔(M⊨ρB) A \equiv B \iff \forall M, \,\forall \rho, \ (M \models_\rho A) \Leftrightarrow (M \models_\rho B) A≡B⟺∀M,∀ρ, (M⊨ρ​A)⇔(M⊨ρ​B)

I.e., AAA and BBB are equivalent (syntactically) iff they are equivalent (semantically) under all interpretations.

Prenex Normal Form

basically we use algebraic identities to bring all quantifiers to the outermost grouping of a formula
every formula has a Prenex form.

useful in (automated) theorem proving.

Standard strategy:

  1. Eliminate →\rightarrow→ and ↔\leftrightarrow↔ (substitute for ¬\lnot¬, ∧\land∧, ∨\lor∨)
  2. Push ¬\lnot¬ inwards (De Morgan’s laws)
  3. Rename variables quantified multiple times (substitution)
  4. Pull quantifiers outwards

Step 3 is necessary since all scopes will be “voided / made global” when the quantifiers are moved to the outermost grouping.
See Common Equivalences above for strategies to achieve each step.
